Q11. What are the risks to global gas prices from this weekend’s developments in the Middle East?

We see significant upside risk to European gas and global LNG prices. BYD officially unveiled the Blade Battery 2.0 on March 5, 2026, introducing an upgraded LMFP (Lithium Manganese Iron Phosphate) battery with energy density of 190–210 Wh/kg. The technology still relies on LFP chemistry, which avoids expensive metals such as cobalt and nickel, allowing significantly lower costs. Current LFP battery packs are estimated at around $81/kWh, compared with roughly $128/kWh for NMC batteries that rely heavily on nickel and cobalt.

BYD spent six years developing the system, with the main challenge being the traditional trade-off between fast charging and energy density. The company’s solution is the FlashPass Ion Transport System, which integrates three technological elements: a “Flash-Release” cathode built on directionally engineered particle structures, a “Flash-Flow” electrolyte optimized using AI for high ionic conductivity, and a “Flash-Intercalate” anode that enables high-speed three-dimensional lithium-ion intercalation.

The result is extremely fast charging performance. The battery can charge from 10% to 70% in about five minutes, and from 10% to 97% in roughly nine minutes. Even under extreme cold conditions between –20°C and –30°C, a 20%–97% charge takes only around 12 minutes, just three minutes longer than under normal temperatures. Using conventional charging infrastructure, the battery still charges 30–50% faster than typical EV batteries. The fastest speeds, however, require BYD’s own 1.5-megawatt Flash Charging stations.

Safety remains a central feature of the Blade architecture. BYD states that the Blade 2.0 can pass multiple nail-penetration tests and complies with China’s latest battery safety regulations, which require that batteries do not catch fire or explode for at least two hours after a single cell enters thermal runaway.

Strategically, BYD is making a clear bet: if LFP batteries can charge fast enough, the traditional advantage of nickel-heavy chemistries such as NMC, which historically offered higher energy density, becomes far less important. In that scenario, cost, durability, and safety become the dominant factors, which favors LFP-based systems.

For the nickel industry, this trend is not particularly encouraging. The rapid improvement in LFP technology continues to erode the share of nickel-intensive batteries in the EV market.

This also raises questions about the valuation of PT Vale Indonesia (INCO). The stock is widely held by domestic institutional investors and currently trades at around 55x PE, while the world’s largest listed nickel producer, Nornickel, trades closer to 15x PE. ## Oil and Gas

The IEA is considering the largest coordinated strategic oil release in history, potentially exceeding the 182 million barrels released in 2022. Let’s run the numbers to understand what this actually means.

Assume the IEA releases 200 million barrels. That roughly offsets what the market has already lost over the past 11 days of disruption, assuming around 20 million barrels per day of crude and products normally moving through the Strait of Hormuz are affected. In other words, the proposed release merely fills the hole that has already formed rather than solving the ongoing disruption if the conflict persists.

It is also worth noting that 200 million barrels is significantly smaller than the 400 million barrels scenario previously discussed as a potential emergency response. In other words, even before considering the mechanics of distribution, the scale of the proposed intervention is already about half of what would be required to meaningfully stabilize supply if the disruption continues.

In theory, the IEA’s 1.8 billion barrels of strategic reserves sound like a massive cushion. If the world were suddenly short 20 million barrels per day, those reserves could offset the deficit for roughly 90 days if every barrel were drained. If Saudi Arabia and the UAE manage to reroute or maintain around 8.8 million barrels per day of supply bypassing Hormuz, the reserves could stretch closer to 160 days.

But the real constraint is not the size of the reserves. It is the rate at which the oil can actually be released. Strategic reserves are stored across dozens of countries and facilities, each with logistical limits on how quickly oil can be withdrawn and transported to market. Current estimates suggest the combined system can release oil at only 5–6 million barrels per day at maximum capacity.

That creates a fundamental mismatch. If the disruption removes 15–20 million barrels per day, the strategic reserves cannot physically replace that flow in real time. At best they slow the drawdown, while shortages propagate across the supply chain. Refiners, petrochemical plants, and fuel distributors begin to feel the pressure long before the reserves meaningfully stabilize supply.

This is why strategic releases often have strong psychological impact but limited structural impact on oil markets during major disruptions. They can smooth temporary shocks, but they cannot replace sustained physical supply losses from a critical chokepoint like Hormuz.

If the conflict drags on and shipping disruptions persist, the market will eventually have to ration demand through price. Under that scenario, the next leg of the adjustment could push crude significantly higher. A move toward $150–$200 per barrel would not be implausible if the disruption becomes prolonged and inventories begin to fall rapidly.

In other words, the proposed release may buy time, but it does not solve the underlying problem. Strategic reserves can cushion the shock for a while, yet they cannot permanently replace the daily flow of oil moving through one of the most important energy corridors in the world. If the conflict endures, the market will ultimately reprice oil to levels high enough to destroy demand and rebalance supply.

## Nickel

Foreign PM: Let’s say BYD succeeds in pushing LFP and LMFP batteries globally and eats into NMC market share. In that scenario nickel gradually becomes obsolete for EV batteries. What happens to the nickel market and to Indonesia, which is the world’s largest nickel producer?

Me: That’s a rich macro question. Let me think through it systematically.

Me: Indonesia today controls roughly 50% of global nickel reserves and has built its entire commodity strategy around the assumption that EV battery demand for nickel, particularly through NMC and NCA chemistries, would be structural and durable. The entire hilirization policy, the smelter buildout, and the HPAL investments were justified by that thesis. If BYD’s LMFP batteries scale globally and LFP takes meaningful market share from NMC, that assumption starts to crack.

Foreign PM: What happens first? The nickel market itself?

Me: The first impact would be structural demand destruction. Stainless steel has always been nickel’s anchor demand, but it is slow growing and commoditized. The real growth story was battery-grade Class 1 nickel, which was expected to absorb a large portion of incremental supply through the 2030s. If LFP wins the EV market, that incremental demand simply disappears.

The market would then face three things at once:
1) Massive oversupply, because Indonesia and the Philippines have aggressively ramped up NPI and HPAL capacity in anticipation of EV demand.
2) Collapse of the Class 1 premium, since battery manufacturers would no longer need high-purity nickel.
3) NPI becomes the global price setter, because stainless steel demand becomes dominant again.

Foreign PM: Meaning nickel prices stay low?

Me: Exactly. Nickel already fell from about $48,000 per tonne in 2022 to roughly $15,000–$16,000 after Indonesian supply flooded the market. If EV demand no longer provides a structural bid, the market could easily settle into a $12,000–$15,000 range or even lower, which is barely above marginal cost for many producers.

Foreign PM: And for Indonesia?

Me: The implications are bigger than the commodity itself. Nickel has become a strategic pillar of Indonesia’s industrial policy.

First, there is a fiscal impact. Government revenues from royalties, export levies, and corporate taxes tied to the nickel complex would fall sharply if prices remain depressed.

Second, the hilirization strategy itself becomes questionable. Indonesia built enormous downstream capacity, much of it financed and operated by Chinese partners, based on the expectation that NMC battery demand would surge. If that demand weakens, Indonesia is left with large refining capacity tied to a shrinking market.

Third, there is a stranded asset problem. HPAL plants, which convert laterite ore into battery-grade nickel sulfate, cost $2–4 billion per facility. They are specifically designed for NMC cathode supply chains. If NMC demand fades, those assets become difficult to repurpose.

Foreign PM: Are there any offsets?

Me: A few, but none are perfect substitutes.

Stainless steel demand will remain, so NPI production continues, but stainless steel alone cannot absorb the supply overhang created by the EV investment cycle.

Another potential offset is manganese, since LMFP batteries use manganese along with iron and phosphate. Indonesia has some manganese resources, but it is not a dominant global producer there, so the upside is limited.

Foreign PM: So the strategic bet becomes questionable.

Me: That’s the key point.

If BYD’s LFP push succeeds globally, Indonesia’s nickel strategy shifts from being a high-conviction industrial policy to a potential macro vulnerability. The country spent a decade building downstream capacity for a demand story that may not materialize at the scale originally envisioned.

The nickel market could then enter a multi-year bear phase, because stainless steel demand alone cannot absorb the supply expansion. At that point the question for Indonesia becomes whether it can diversify its commodity base and industrial strategy quickly enough, or whether the nickel boom ultimately becomes a cautionary example of a resource nation that downstream-integrated into the wrong technology cycle at the wrong time.
